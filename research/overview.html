Phase 1: Visual Neuroscience

Deciphering the Neural Code

Before we can correct vision, we must understand how the brain processes it. Our research begins at the foundational level: analyzing how visual pathways interpret motion and contrast.

Motion Contrast Sensitivity in Visual Pathways

Investigating Hemispheric Asymmetry via Hemianopia Simulation

Does being right-handed mean your "right visual brain" is also dominant? We simulated hemianopia (blindness in half the visual field) to test the detection thresholds for stationary and moving stimuli. This foundational work helps us understand the limits of visual processing.

Methodology

  • Subjects: 24 adults (21 Right-handed, 3 Left-handed)
  • Task: Detect 1 cpd Gabor patches
  • Variable: Speed (0, 10, 30 cm/s)

Key Insight

Sensitivity drops significantly as speed increases (p<.001), but surprisingly, hemispheric dominance (language) does not directly correlate with low-level visual processing.

Phase 2: Optical Engineering

Controlling the Eye through Light

We don't just correct vision; we actively manage eye growth. By analyzing the peripheral retina's focal plane, we develop optical interventions to stop myopia progression.

Full-Retina Total Correction

The End of Myopia Progression

Traditional glasses correct only the center of vision. Our research shows that peripheral defocus drives the eye to grow longer (myopia). We are developing a "Total Correction" platform that maps the entire retina and adjusts the focal plane to inhibit this growth.

The Problem

FS' (Sagittal Focal Line) behind the retina triggers axial elongation (eye growth).

The Observation

As myopia worsens, the nasal retina becomes steeper, pushing the focal line further back.

The Solution

Induced myopic defocus in the periphery effectively signals the eye to stop growing.
